## DELIVERABLE 1 — Website Strategy Summary

### What Is Wrong with the Current Site Structure

The current FMC website was built to get something online quickly — and it shows. The core structural problems are:

**Flat, undifferentiated navigation.** All top-level tabs carry equal visual weight with no hierarchy. This means product content, company information, and news sit side by side with no clear entry points for different audiences (investors, engineers, partners, media).

**No segmented news strategy.** The site mixes FMC-authored news, external industry coverage, and internal updates with no categorical distinction. Visitors can't quickly filter to what's relevant for them — an investor wants press coverage, a recruiter wants company culture, an engineer wants technical announcements.

**About FMC lacks depth.** The current About section offers no sub-structure for Leadership, Board of Directors, or Industry Advisors — three types of stakeholders that signal credibility to investors and partners. These should be distinct, browsable sections.

**No dedicated Careers presence.** Recruiting top semiconductor talent in a competitive market requires more than a tab. FMC needs a Careers section that conveys culture, departments, open roles, and a clear hiring process.

**Inconsistent visual hierarchy.** Content blocks compete for attention. There is no consistent use of eyebrow labels, section cadence, or CTA structure to guide a visitor through the intended journey.

---

### Why Arm Is a Good Inspiration Reference

Arm (arm.com) is one of the most well-respected corporate technology websites in the semiconductor industry. It works as a reference for FMC for specific structural reasons — not for visual style or branding.

- **Mega menus signal enterprise credibility.** Arm's mega menus organize deep content into discoverable columns with featured cards. This communicates that the company has substance — multiple products, resources, teams — and makes that depth accessible at a glance.

- **Clear corporate hierarchy.** Arm separates Technology, Solutions, Resources, Company, and Careers as distinctly different pillar categories. Each serves a different audience and has a different depth of content. FMC should mirror this pillar logic.

- **News and Resources are distinct from Marketing.** Arm treats content as a structured resource library, not just a blog feed. This signals maturity to analysts, investors, and technical buyers.

- **About/Company sectioning.** Arm's company section has distinct sub-pages for Leadership, Board, Investors, Sustainability, and Careers — all of which are individually findable. This is the model FMC's About FMC section should follow.

The key principle: borrow Arm's *structural logic*, not its visual identity. FMC should look like FMC — dark, technical, green — but be organized like a company with Arm-level credibility.

---

### How the Proposed Structure Improves FMC's Website

The proposed redesign addresses each structural problem directly:

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Flat navigation | 6 pillar top-level nav with mega menus on Technology and About FMC |
| Undifferentiated news | News & Insights hub with 5 tabs: FMC News, Industry Mentions, FMC Insider, Events, Media Resources |
| Shallow About FMC | Dedicated sub-sections: Company Overview, Leadership Team, Board of Directors, Industry Advisors, Locations |
| No Careers section | Full Careers page: Life at FMC, Why Join Us, Departments, Open Positions, Hiring Process, Talent Network |
| Weak visual hierarchy | Consistent eyebrow/headline/subheadline/CTA structure across every section |
| No audience routing | Contact page with 6 distinct pathways for different visitor types |

---

### Why This Should Be Framed as Professionalization, Not Rebrand

FMC has a distinctive brand — the dark green palette, the logo, the technical tone. None of that changes in this proposal. What changes is *structure and clarity*, not identity.

This matters for internal alignment: proposing a rebrand invites debate about colors, logos, and tone. Proposing a professionalization invites agreement — because the argument is simply that the current site makes it harder to find things and signals less credibility than the company deserves.

Frame it this way: "We are not changing what FMC looks like. We are making sure it looks as professional as the technology it represents."

## DELIVERABLE 6 — Manager Presentation Summary

**Subject: FMC Website Professionalization — Concept Proposal**

---

I have completed a review of the current FMC website and developed a professional redesign concept. Here is a summary of the work and recommendations.

**What I reviewed**

I reviewed the current FMC website structure, identified the key structural weaknesses, and benchmarked it against Arm (arm.com) — one of the most well-regarded corporate websites in the semiconductor industry — as an inspiration reference for navigation structure and content hierarchy.

**What I recommend**

*1. Reorganize the navigation into 6 clear pillars*
Technology / Applications / News & Insights / About FMC / Careers / Contact — each serving a distinct audience and content need. The Technology and About FMC tabs should use mega menus to make their depth immediately visible and accessible.

*2. Create a segmented News & Insights hub*
Rather than a flat news feed, introduce 5 distinct content categories: FMC News, Industry Mentions, FMC Insider, Events, and Media Resources. This lets investors, engineers, media, and recruits each find the content most relevant to them. It also gives the communications team a structured framework for releasing new content over time.

*3. Expand About FMC into structured sub-sections*
About FMC should have clearly navigable sub-pages for: Company Overview, Leadership Team, Board of Directors, Industry Advisors, and Locations. This signals governance depth and builds trust with investors and strategic partners.

*4. Build a proper Careers section*
The Careers section should convey culture and structure, not just list open jobs. Recommended sections: Life at FMC, Why Join Us, Departments, Open Positions, a transparent Hiring Process, and a Talent Network CTA for passive candidates.

*5. Introduce a structured Contact page*
Replace a generic contact form with 6 clearly labelled contact pathways: Request a Demo, Schedule a Technology Briefing, Partner with FMC, Media Inquiry, Careers/Talent, and General Contact. Each serves a different visitor intent.

**What stays the same**

This is a professionalization, not a rebrand. The FMC logo, color palette (`#0B1511`, `#56B686`, `#7BDCB5`, `#00D084`), and technical tone remain entirely unchanged. The redesign simply ensures the website is as structured and credible as the technology it represents.

I have produced a fully working static website mockup (HTML/CSS/JS) with all 6 pages, working navigation including mega menus, responsive mobile layout, and the full FMC color system applied consistently throughout. This can be opened directly in any browser as a reference for the development team or external agency.
